The "Living Standard" Checklist

This is the core mechanism for making governance operational. It's a single, dynamic standard we apply in two distinct ways to connect our strategy to execution for every digital asset.

Approach A: The "Health Check" For Existing Digital Assets

Goal: To systematically diagnose our current environment, identify gaps, and create a prioritized remediation plan. The checklist is our diagnostic tool.

Checklist Structure by Pillar:

We assess every key asset against these questions within our central EA Tool.

  • Business: What is its criticality? Which capability does it support?
  • Data: What is its data classification? Does it comply with residency laws?
  • Application: Does it use standard SSO? Is it built on an approved pattern?
  • Technology: Is it on a supported OS? Is it in an approved cloud landing zone?
  • Governance: Who is the owner? When was the last security review?
Approach B: The "Quality Gates" For New, Enhanced, and Maintained Assets

Goal: To prevent gaps from ever being created by embedding governance directly into the asset lifecycle. The checklist becomes our quality criteria.

Lifecycle Tollgate Criteria:

Our checklist is applied at key decision points. Click each stage to see the details.

A "light" checklist is used to assess initial risk, data sensitivity, and architectural fit before project approval. This forces early strategic thinking.

The full, detailed checklist is used by the Architecture Review Board (ARB) as the scorecard to approve the technical design. No development starts without this approval.

A final verification checklist is used by the Change Advisory Board (CAB) to ensure security tests are passed, support teams are trained, and the system is operationally ready for production.

The Smart Engine: Dynamic, Tiered Checklists

This is the key to being efficient, not bureaucratic. The rigor of the checklist automatically adapts to the risk of the asset.

Step 1: Define Tiers (Input)

We ask two simple questions at the start:

"What is the Business Criticality?"

"What is the Data Classification?"

Step 2: Generate Checklist (Output)

The system presents the appropriate checklist based on the risk profile.

Example: Low-Risk Asset
An internal, Tier 3 project with Public data gets a lightweight 15-point checklist focused on basics like ownership and registration. This enables speed.
Example: High-Risk Asset
A customer-facing, Tier 1 application with Confidential data gets the full 100-point checklist with mandatory sections for security, privacy, and resilience. This ensures rigor.
