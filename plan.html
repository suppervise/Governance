Foundational Architecture Plan

For CGS as a Conglomerate Group: Ensuring Standards, Security, and Maximum Efficiency

Implementation Steps

To ensure comprehensive management of IT resources while aligning IT initiatives with global standards for robustness, high availability, and resilience, several IT governance frameworks stand out. Each has unique strengths and can complement each other depending on our organization's needs.

Framework Focus Area Strengths
COBIT IT Governance Aligns IT with business goals; risk management; performance measurement
ITIL Service Management Service lifecycle management; enhances service quality; best practices
ISO/IEC 38500 Governance Principles International standard; flexible application; risk monitoring
TOGAF Enterprise Architecture Structured approach for architecture; alignment with business strategies
Additional Consideration: The real power comes from integrating these, not just picking one. For example, COBIT provides the "what" (governance objectives), ITIL provides the "how" for service management, ISO 38500 provides high-level principles, and TOGAF provides the "how" for architecture.Integrating the strengths of each framework, including ISO 27001 (Information Security) and NIST CSF (Cybersecurity), will strengthen overall governance.

Ensure the framework remains effective and focuses on three key areas: setting mandatory standards, implementing processes for conformance, and conducting regular cross-checks for the entire Conglomerate Group (CGS). Consider the approach for the existing system.

2.1 Setting Mandatory Standards

Objective: Establish clear, non-compromise guidelines for IT practices across the organization.

How to Simplify:

  • Prioritize critical areas: Focus on standards for key domains like application, data, technology, infrastructure, compliance and Identify Critical Business Drivers: What are the most important business objectives that IT must support? (e.g., resilience, high availability).
  • Leverage existing frameworks: Instead of reinventing the wheel, adopt well-established standards from frameworks like COBIT, ITIL, ISO 27001, or NIST for areas like security, availability, and compliance.
  • Document and Communicate: Make standards easily accessible (e.g., an intranet site) with clear explanations and tailored for CGS so they’re easy to understand and follow.
2.2 Implementing Processes to Ensure Conformance

Objective: Ensure that all IT activities align with the established standards without adding unnecessary complexity.

How to Simplify:

  • Embed standards in workflows: Integrate compliance checks into daily IT operations (e.g., build approvals into DevOps pipelines, use automated tools for change management, and enforce policies through configuration management tools).
  • Streamline governance workflows: Simplify approval processes for changes, vendor onboarding, or new technology adoption to prevent bureaucratic delays while still maintaining control.
  • Train and empower staff: Provide regular training so employees understand the standards and processes. Empower teams to take ownership of compliance in their specific areas to decentralize responsibility.
  • Automate compliance monitoring and suggestion: Use monitoring tools or AI that flag non-compliance in real-time (e.g., automated alerts for system downtime, non-patched vulnerabilities, or unapproved software) and suggest the best practice.
2.3 Regular Cross-Checks and Alignment

Objective: Ensure ongoing adherence to IT standards through consistent and efficient auditing.

How to Simplify:

  • Focus on critical areas: Prioritize audits on high-risk areas (security, business continuity, data protection) rather than auditing everything, ensuring efficient use of resources.
  • Regular Audits: Conduct periodic audits (e.g., quarterly or annually) to assess compliance with standards. This could include:
    • Technical Audits: Reviewing system configurations, code, and security controls.
    • Process Audits: Verifying that IT processes (e.g., change management, incident response) are being followed.

Example Detail for a Domain:

3.1 Application Architecture

Objective: Design, develop, and maintain robust, scalable, and secure applications that align with business needs.

Roles and Responsibilities:

  • Design application systems that align with business goals.
  • Ensure interoperability between applications, infrastructure, and data.
  • Evaluate and integrate new technologies that improve application functionality.
  • Establish guidelines for coding, testing, and deployment to ensure consistency and quality.

SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures):

  • Application design reviews: Regular design sessions to ensure adherence to architecture principles. Defines the application architecture, including technology stack, integration points, and design patterns.
  • Change management procedures: Control processes for application updates, versioning, and testing before deployment.
  • Dependency mapping: Maintain an updated list of application dependencies to minimize disruptions during changes.

Control Mechanisms:

  • Architecture review team: A governance committee to approve application designs and changes.
  • New application coming gates: Consider a business alignment, redundancy with other app, appropriate of development technology and Vendor selection.

Reference Standards:

  • TOGAF ADM: Use for application portfolio management, mapping applications to business capabilities, and defining application architecture blueprints.
  • OWASP Application Security Testing Guide: Use for secure coding practices and vulnerability management.
  • Agile Development Methodologies (e.g., Scrum, Kanban): For iterative and collaborative application development.
  • ISO/IEC 25010: Software quality models for ensuring reliability and usability.
  • CMMI (Capability Maturity Model Integration): For continuous process improvement.
Note: It is necessary to expand the details for the other four domains (Data Architecture, Technology Architecture, Governance & Compliance, Business Architecture) in a similar manner, covering roles, responsibilities, SOPs, control mechanisms, and reference standards for each.

Benchmarking and Gap Analysis:
  • Select Relevant SOPs to use as benchmarks for our assessment.
  • Conduct Gap Analysis: Compare our current state of IT governance against the CGS’s SOP to identify areas for improvement.
Prioritized Assessment:
  • Critical Systems First: Focus on assessing projects and systems that are most critical to business operations.
  • Risk-Based Approach: Prioritize assessments based on the potential impact and likelihood of non-compliance.
Plan to close gap:
  • Address Prioritized Gaps: Create specific action items to address the prioritized gaps identified in the assessment phase.
  • Assign Responsibilities: Clearly assign ownership for each action item and define roles and responsibilities for implementing the improvements.
  • Set Timelines and Milestones: Establish realistic timelines for completing each action item and define key milestones for tracking progress.
  • Resource Allocation: Allocate the necessary resources (budget, staff time, tools) to support the implementation of the action plan.

  • Prioritize and Execute Remediation Projects: Use the blueprints to guide the prioritization and execution of projects to close gaps and move towards the target state.
  • Establish Continuous Monitoring: Implement monitoring and reporting mechanisms to track progress, identify new gaps, and ensure ongoing alignment with standards.
  • Iterate and Improve: IT governance is an ongoing process. Regularly review and update blueprints, standards, and processes based on changing business needs, technology advancements, and lessons learned.
Key Success Factor: Fostering a Continuous Improvement Culture and ensuring participation from all stakeholders will make IT governance sustainable and responsive to change.

Our Strategic Roadmap for Governance and Excellence

A long-term, cyclical process to close gaps and prevent future risk.

1. Establish Mandatory Standards

Define clear, practical, and mandatory standards for Application, Data, and Technology architecture, forming the 'rulebook' for all digital initiatives.

2. Create Comprehensive Visibility

Partner with local IT across all subsidiaries to map and document every digital asset (apps, data, networks) in our central Enterprise Architecture repository.

3. Analyze Gaps Against Standards

Systematically compare our existing asset inventory against the mandatory standards to perform a data-driven gap analysis, identifying risk and technical debt.

4. Plan and Prioritize Remediation

Develop a risk-based plan to close identified gaps. Prioritize initiatives that reduce the most critical risks and deliver the highest business value.

5. Embed Governance into Workflows

Integrate our standards directly into project lifecycle and change management processes, making compliance the default and preventing future gaps.

6. Monitor, Audit, and Automate

Implement continuous monitoring of our digital landscape, leveraging automation and AI to detect anomalies and ensure sustained compliance and resilience.
